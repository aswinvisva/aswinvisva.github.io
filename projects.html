ConvoBuddy,
Making Conversations Easier.

People with autism have a difficult time identifying emotions and engaging in social conversations. Our product is designed to help such individuals gain feedback about their ability to maintain eye contact and identify emotion from real conversations.

Our product uses a Raspberry Pi powered by Google's Cloud Vision API to determine the emotions of individuals in the capture and also the level of eye contact. This information is then pushed to a Firebase database which is referenced an iOS app used to display the information.

We were able to earn the Best Use of Google Cloud Platform during the QHacks 2019 Hackathon.

QuickSense,
Find the Things You Need.

The team was inspired by patients at the Canadian Hellen Keller center who called for more innovations in technology targeted towards individuals with special needs.

QuickSense is a glove designed for individuals who are blind or partially sighted to aid them in finding everyday objects around the house, such as a spoon or fork. The glove is equipped with a camera module and a Raspberry Pi microcomputer running an object recognition algorithm backed by OpenCV.

This project was developed for the StarterHacks 2019 Hackathon.

Sign Language Translating Glove,
breaking language barriers.

In Canada, there are around 3 million hard of hearing individuals according to the Canadian Association of the Deaf. These individuals often have difficulty carrying out simple tasks such as ordering a drink at a restaurant or asking for help due to the majority of the population lacking the ability to communicate through American Sign Language. In the eleventh grade, Sultan Emaish and I created a device which could bridge the gap between English speakers and those who communicate through American Sign Language.

We created a glove which used flex sensors, an accelerometer and an algorithm which sorted these sensor values to determine the corresponding English word. We received feedback from parents and staff at the school wide science fair and improved our solution, making the glove more compact and ergonomic.

Towards the end of the year, we participated in the Toronto Science Fair, collecting a gold medal and a scholarship for Best Senior Project. A few weeks later, we showcased our design to hearing impaired individuals at the Canadian Helen Keller centre and it was very well received, this provided us with the motivation to continue this project.

In twelfth grade, we pitched this idea to Hadi Salah who works for a business incubator centre at the Pitch your Tech event and sparked the interest of CBC news, who contacted us through the youth counselor at our school.

Screen Time Calculator,
helping build healthy screen time habits.

Across the world, individuals are spending too much time on their devices and as a result are harming their health and social lives. I created a program for my Grade 12 Computer Science culminating to help individuals be aware of their screen time usage.

The program used the OpenCV facial recognition library to measure the user's amount of time spent in front of the screen and warned the user to take a break when the screen time approached 20 minutes.

I built a graphic user interface with Java and XML to provide warnings to the user and to update the data real-time.
